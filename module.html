Brief instruction on key fluency features

To help you sound more natural and fluent in English, this module focuses on features that native and proficient speakers use automatically.

Connected speech features
Fluent speech isn’t about pronouncing each word separately and clearly – it’s about a series of smooth and natural sound adjustments.

  1. Consonant‑Vowel (CV) linking
    When a word ending in a consonant is followed by a word starting with a vowel, native speakers connect them smoothly, e.g., ‘an apple’ sounds like ‘a‑napple’, ‘turn off’ sounds like ‘tur‑noff’.
  2. Weak forms of function words
    Common function words (like ‘to’, ‘of’, ‘can’, ‘for’) are usually pronounced in their weak forms with vowel reduced as /ə/ ‘uh’ sound in natural speech.
  3. Elision (sound deletion)
    In rapid, natural speech, certain sounds disappear completely:
    ‘next week’ → the /t/ disappears: ‘nex(t) week’
    ‘heard him’ → the /h/ in ‘him’ disappears: ‘heard (h)im’

Temporal features
How fast you speak and where you pause are equally important for fluency.

  1. Speech rate
    Fluent speakers maintain a comfortable, natural pace – not too fast (which suggests nervousness) and not too slow (which suggests hesitation or word‑searching). Aim for smooth, steady delivery with appropriate rhythm.
  2. Pausing
    Strategic pausing is the mark of confident speakers. Fluent speakers pause:
    • at natural phrase boundaries (not in the middle of phrases);
    • to organize thoughts (brief, purposeful pauses);
    • for emphasis or effect;
    • avoid excessive hesitation pauses filled with ‘um’, ‘uh’, or long silences mid‑sentence.
